# Elicitation template — ENISA evidence-body counterparty

> Internal working template for ENISA review / evidence-body interactions / regulatory-context engagements. ENISA-style counterparties expect formal documentation, source-graded evidence, and bidirectional questioning. The internal-register baseline is critical: ENISA reads our **external register** (formal, evidence-based) but our internal preparation is HUMINT-grounded.

## Counterparty baseline reality assessment

| Field | Value |
|:---|:---|
| Counterparty name (anonymised) | `[ENISA-Officer-NN]` |
| Review type | grant evaluation / regulatory verification / compliance audit / technical-panel review |
| Reviewer specialisation | technical / financial / regulatory / mixed |
| Review history (declared) | what they've said about prior similar reviews they've conducted |
| Review history (inferred) | from public ENISA reports + reviewer publication patterns |
| Decision-criteria (declared) | the explicit evaluation criteria of the review |
| Decision-criteria (inferred) | what the criteria really weigh based on past outcomes |
| Adversarial-stance threshold | how much push-back to expect; reviewers vary widely |
| Time-discipline | review windows are firm; missing a deadline = restart |

## Approach techniques (planned)

| Technique | Specific to ENISA | Rationale |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Direct interrogation | OFTEN INVITED | ENISA review is inherently structured questioning; accept the frame |
| Direct elicitation | YES | When the reviewer extends conversational space beyond pure interrogation |
| Indirect elicitation | RARE | ENISA reviewers expect direct answers; indirect reads as evasive |
| Provocation | NEVER | Adversarial-by-design context; provocation reads as obstruction |

## Source grading

ENISA reviews are evidence-body engagements; **every claim in the response must have a graded source**. Sources commonly consulted: prior ENISA grant reports, public-domain reviewer publications, our own prior submissions (if any), industry-standard methodology references.

## Discovery questions (we're being interrogated; this section captures **what we want to learn FROM the reviewer** during the engagement, not what they ask us)

### Section A — Frame-setting

1. *Confirm scope* — "Help us confirm we understand the evaluation framework: criteria [X, Y, Z], evidence weight [high/medium/low per criterion]."

### Section B — Direct elicitation (about the review process)

2. What's the evidence type that has carried the most weight in similar reviews you've conducted?
3. Where do submissions in our category most often fall short?
4. How does the panel reconcile divergent reviewer assessments?

### Section C — Indirect elicitation (about the criteria)

5. Of recent submissions in our space that received [funding/approval/etc.], what was the evidence pattern that distinguished them?
6. What would a "best-in-class" submission for our category look like?

### Section D — Reverse-elicitation (probe for unstated criteria)

7. We've identified [criterion X] as a likely concern; do you anticipate the panel will probe deeper on this?
8. We've explicitly addressed [common-failure-mode]; is there a related concern we should pre-empt?

### Section E — Closing

9. What's the timeline window for follow-up clarifications? When do we hear back?

## Post-engagement reporting

Intelligence report focused on:
- BLUF on likely review outcome.
- Source-graded reliability of every reviewer-stated criterion (most are A-1 or B-1; ENISA reviewers are inherently reliable but their **personal interpretations** of criteria may be C-2 or B-2).
- Updated baseline reality on the specific reviewer and panel.
- Recommended next action: prepare follow-up clarifications / submit revised version / proceed to next stage.
